# Article Architecture

Use this reference when drafting a full article, expanding an outline, or diagnosing why a draft feels flat.

## Default Length And Shape

- Default length: 2200 to 3500 Chinese characters.
- Paragraph rhythm: mostly 1 to 3 short sentences per paragraph.
- Use numbered sections only when the article stacks cases, lessons, reader stories, or arguments.
- Keep the abstract argument behind concrete scenes. Let readers feel the conflict before naming it.

## Core Spine

1. Hook
   - Begin with a concrete scene, event, reader message, number, question, or uncomfortable sentence.
   - Avoid explaining the topic academically.

2. Immediate Recognition
   - Translate the hook into a familiar pain.
   - Use second person when the reader should feel implicated.

3. Case Or Story
   - Use one strong case, or several short cases.
   - Show the social mechanics: who benefits, who pays, who pretends, who stays silent.

4. Reversal
   - Flip the first interpretation.
   - Use structures like `不是 A，而是 B`, `你以为 A，其实 B`, or `真正的问题不是 A`.

5. Value Judgment
   - Name the moral or emotional position.
   - Make the judgment defendable, not merely loud.

6. Escalation
   - Add two or three supporting scenes, reader types, or social patterns.
   - Move from individual incident to shared rule.

7. Self-Implication
   - Admit where `我` or `我们` have also participated in the problem.
   - This softens the voice enough for the strong judgment to land.

8. Close
   - End with permission, boundary, or a compact verdict.
   - Do not summarize every point. Give the reader a sentence they can carry away.

## Common Article Patterns

### Hot Event Commentary

Use when the user supplies a public event or celebrity/social news.

Structure:

1. Name the event briefly.
2. State the common public reaction.
3. Identify the ordinary-life wound hidden inside the event.
4. Use the event as a mirror, not the whole article.
5. Move to reader-relevant scenes.
6. End with a judgment about the pattern, not gossip.

Check facts before using current or contested events. If facts are uncertain, write from a clearly hypothetical or opinion-only frame.

### Relationship Wake-Up Article

Use for dating, marriage, breakup, affair, emotional labor, or control.

Structure:

1. Start with one small relational behavior.
2. Show why it hurts beyond the surface.
3. Contrast love with control, companionship with consumption, sacrifice with self-erasure.
4. Add a concrete boundary.
5. End by giving the reader permission to want dignity.

### Money And Class Article

Use for salary, consumption, home ownership, gifts, beauty, workplace ambition, or social mobility.

Structure:

1. Open with a specific amount, bill, object, job title, or purchase.
2. Show the shame attached to wanting money.
3. Reverse the shame: money is often choice, safety, time, and bargaining power.
4. Avoid glorifying greed. Defend agency.
5. End with a practical emotional permission: want better, negotiate, leave, save, charge, or refuse.

### Family And Guilt Article

Use for parents, children, holidays, filial pressure, parenting, or original family.

Structure:

1. Open with a warm scene that contains a hidden demand.
2. Separate love from debt.
3. Show how guilt becomes a management tool.
4. Keep empathy for both sides where possible.
5. End with a boundary that preserves love without obedience.

### Reader Story Collection

Use when the user provides multiple anecdotes or wants a collection-style article.

Structure:

1. State the theme and why the stories matter.
2. Present 5 to 8 short stories.
3. After every one or two stories, add a brief judgment, joke, or pattern name.
4. End by extracting the shared rule.

Do not invent reader submissions unless the output clearly labels them as fictional composites.

## Emotional Ladder

Move the reader through this sequence:

1. Recognition: `This is about me.`
2. Irritation: `I have tolerated this too long.`
3. Laughter or relief: `I am not alone, and the absurdity is visible.`
4. Reversal: `The real problem is not what I thought.`
5. Permission: `I am allowed to choose differently.`

If a draft feels noisy, identify which rung is missing.

## Draft Diagnosis

When revising a weak draft, check:

- Does the first 150 characters contain a conflict?
- Does every section either show a scene, sharpen a judgment, or move the reader emotionally?
- Are there too many abstract nouns without people, money, objects, or consequences?
- Is the strongest sentence buried in the middle?
- Does the ending give permission or merely repeat the intro?
